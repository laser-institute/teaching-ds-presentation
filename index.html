Teaching Data Science to Educational Researchers

Evidence-Based Strategies for Effective Pedagogy

Based on research by K. Bret Staudt Willet (Florida State University) & Joshua M. Rosenberg (University of Tennessee)

Journal of Formative Design in Learning, Dec. 2023

Understanding Your Students: The Learning Context

The Educational Data Landscape

Educational researchers increasingly encounter data from:

  • Learning Management Systems (LMS)
  • Online and distance education platforms
  • AI-powered learning tools

This has created demand for Educational Data Science (EDS) skills to analyze these data sources for research and practice.

Barriers to Learning Data Science

Educational researchers face several challenges:

  • Ethical considerations: Navigating consent and privacy requirements
  • Access limitations: Computing resources and restricted datasets
  • Technical concerns: Anxiety about coding and statistical software
For instructors: These barriers present pedagogical challenges that require thoughtful instructional design.

What We Studied: Key Questions for Instructors

This research explored how to effectively teach data science to educational researchers. As instructors, we need to understand:

RQ #1: Who are your students?

How do educational researchers situate themselves concerning data science? What backgrounds, motivations, and fears do they bring to your classroom?

RQ #2: What teaching strategies work?

How can instructional design help educational researchers overcome barriers to learning data science? What pedagogical approaches build confidence and competence?

For You as Instructors: These findings will help you design more effective learning experiences for your students.

Participants brought relevant experience to the workshops:

57%

Student motivations:

  • 77% sought to develop new analytical skills
  • 75% wanted skills applicable to their current work
Implication for instruction: Students are not novices to research or data analysis. Effective instruction connects new computational methods to their existing knowledge of educational research and statistical concepts.

Technical Anxiety as a Learning Barrier

Despite relevant backgrounds, participants reported significant concerns about technical aspects:

84%
Cited technical factors as primary concern

Representative student comments:

"I really do not understand the statistical stuff. I might as well be reading Russian."
"Some of the programs, such as R, are a little intimidating."
Instructional consideration: While students' concerns center on technical content, pedagogical approaches can address these anxieties by creating supportive learning environments that normalize the learning process.

Effective Instructional Approaches

Students identified pedagogical factors as most beneficial to their learning:

66%
Cited pedagogical factors as workshop strength

Specific teaching strategies that supported learning:

  • Patience and flexibility: Adjusting pace and providing multiple explanations
  • Collaborative coding: Working through problems together
  • Normalizing errors: Treating mistakes as learning opportunities
  • Plain language explanations: Connecting code to familiar concepts
"It was helpful to practice... I also really appreciated when you explained what the code was saying [by] reading it out loud."
"[The instructor] made a 'bunch of numbers' actually mean something."

Five Evidence-Based Instructional Guidelines

Based on this research, we developed five guidelines for teaching data science to educational researchers.

5

Research-Based Guidelines

These guidelines emerged from analysis of student feedback and learning outcomes, providing evidence-based recommendations for instruction.

Instructional Guidelines for Data Science Education

1. Acknowledge and Build on Prior Knowledge

Connect new computational methods to students' existing research experience and statistical knowledge. Avoid assumptions about their starting knowledge level.

2. Use Familiar, Educational Data

Begin with datasets that reflect educational contexts (e.g., student assessment data, survey responses) rather than generic examples from other fields.

3. Establish Clear Connections to Research Practice

Explicitly link each technique to educational research questions and demonstrate applications relevant to students' work contexts.

4. Create Supportive Learning Environments

Normalize the debugging process, model problem-solving approaches, and maintain patience when students encounter difficulties.

5. Balance Instruction with Guided Practice

Structure lessons with demonstration, collaborative work, and individual application opportunities rather than extended lectures.

Implications for Instruction

Effective data science instruction can broaden participation in educational research.

This research demonstrates that thoughtful pedagogical approaches can address common barriers to learning data science among educational researchers.

Key findings for instructors:

  • Students bring valuable research and analytical experience that can be leveraged
  • Technical anxiety is a significant but addressable barrier
  • Pedagogical approaches are more important than specific software choices
  • Confidence can increase substantially with appropriate instructional support
Instructional design can determine accessibility of data science skills in education
